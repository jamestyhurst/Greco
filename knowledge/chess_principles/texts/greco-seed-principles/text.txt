Chess Principles — Greco Reference Notes

These are short, original notes on enduring chess principles, written for the
Greco project and released into the public domain (CC0). They exist so Greco's
retrieval layer has correct material to work with before the classical
public-domain books are added. They may be quoted freely.


On development and the opening.

The opening is a race to mobilize your forces. Bring out your knights and bishops
toward the center before you commit your queen or launch a pawn storm. A piece
that still sits on its starting square is a piece that is not yet playing the
game. Do not move the same piece twice in the opening without a concrete reason,
because every wasted tempo is a move your opponent spends developing while you
stand still. Castle early: a king left in the center is a target, and the rook
you connect by castling is a piece brought to life.


On the center.

Control of the central squares — e4, d4, e5, d5 — is control of the board,
because a piece in the center radiates influence in every direction while a piece
on the rim reaches only half as far. A knight on the edge of the board attacks
fewer squares than a knight in the middle; this is the grain of truth behind the
old saying that a knight on the rim is dim. Claim the center with pawns when you
can, and support those pawns with pieces.


On king safety.

Most attacks succeed because the defender neglected the safety of the king.
Castle before you open lines, not after. When you have castled, be slow to push
the pawns in front of your king, because each pawn you advance is a hook the
opponent can grab and a hole he can occupy. If you must weaken the king's shelter,
be sure you are getting something concrete in return.


On the sacrifice.

A sacrifice is the deliberate surrender of material for something you judge to be
worth more — an attack, a fork, a passed pawn, a mating net. The soundness of a
sacrifice is not decided by whether it looks brave but by whether the
consequences can be calculated to a favorable end. The mark of a strong player is
the willingness to give up material when vision says the resulting position is
winning, and the discipline to decline a sacrifice that is mere hope dressed up as
courage. Count first, then leap.


On the fork and the double attack.

The double attack is the engine of chess tactics: one move that creates two
threats, so that the opponent can answer only one. A knight is the natural forking
piece because it strikes in a pattern no other piece can block or see coming, but
pawns, bishops, rooks, and queens all fork as well. The pattern to train your eye
for is two enemy pieces a single knight-move apart from a square you can reach, or
two valuable pieces on the same line a pawn can split. Watch your own pieces for
the same geometry, for a fork you allow is as real as one you find.


On the pin and the skewer.

A pin freezes a piece because moving it would expose a more valuable piece behind
it; a skewer is the same geometry in reverse, forcing the valuable piece to move
and abandon the one behind. Both only win material when the line is real — when
the piece in front truly cannot move without loss. Pieces merely sharing a rank,
file, or diagonal are not pinned; check whether the capture actually wins before
you trust the pin. A pin against the king is absolute, for the king may never
legally step into capture.


On pawn structure and the doubled pawn.

Pawns cannot move backward, so every pawn move is permanent and every pawn
weakness is a debt that may come due many moves later. Doubled pawns — two pawns
of one color on the same file — cannot defend each other and often cannot advance,
yet the half-open file they create can be worth more than the structural damage if
your rooks use it. An isolated pawn, with no friendly pawn on either neighboring
file, must be guarded by pieces rather than pawns; it is both a weakness to
besiege and, because of the open lines around it, a source of activity for its
owner. Judge a structure by what it enables, not only by what it looks like.


On the open file and the seventh rank.

Rooks are long-range pieces that do nothing behind their own pawns and everything
on an open file. Seize an open file with a rook, double your rooks upon it if you
can, and aim to reach the seventh rank, where a rook attacks the enemy pawns from
behind and pens in the enemy king. A rook on the seventh is often worth a pawn or
more of pure activity, because it makes threats faster than the defender can meet
them.


On the endgame and the activity of the king.

The endgame inverts one rule of the opening and middlegame: the king, hidden away
while the queens are on, becomes a fighting piece once they leave the board. Bring
the king toward the center and toward the action; a passive king in the endgame is
a piece down. In rook endings, activity outweighs a pawn more often than not — an
active rook behind a passed pawn, and a king marching to support its own passer,
will frequently save or win a position that a materialist count calls lost. Place
the rook behind the passed pawn, whether it is yours or your opponent's.


On the passed pawn.

A passed pawn — one with no enemy pawn ahead of it on its file or the files beside
it — is a candidate to promote, and the threat of promotion grows with every
square it advances. A passed pawn must be pushed, the saying goes, because a
runner on the seventh rank ties down enemy pieces that would rather be elsewhere.
But push it with support; a passed pawn that outruns its own pieces is merely a
target. The endgame is, very often, the story of who can make a passed pawn first.


On the two bishops.

A pair of bishops, working together on light and dark squares, covers the whole
board and grows stronger as the position opens and pawns come off. When you hold
the two bishops, seek to open lines; when you face them, keep the position closed
and look to trade one of them for a knight. The advantage of the two bishops is
small but durable, the kind of edge that decides a quiet game.
