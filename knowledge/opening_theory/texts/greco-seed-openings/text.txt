Opening Principles and Named Openings — Greco Reference Notes

Short, original notes on opening play and the character of a few classical
openings, written for the Greco project and released into the public domain (CC0).
They exist so Greco's retrieval layer has correct opening material before the
classical public-domain opening books are added. They may be quoted freely.


The aims of the opening.

Every sound opening serves the same three aims: develop the pieces quickly, fight
for the center, and bring the king to safety by castling. The named openings are
simply the well-charted roads by which strong players have pursued those aims for
centuries. Knowing an opening's name matters less than understanding its ideas —
which pawn breaks it is built around, which pieces want which squares, and what
kind of middlegame it is steering toward.


The Italian Game (1.e4 e5 2.Nf3 Nc3 3.Bc4).

One of the oldest openings, the Italian develops a knight and a bishop toward the
center and eyes the sensitive f7 square. It is a natural classical setup: claim
the center with the king's pawn, develop with threats, and castle. The Giuoco
Piano — the "quiet game" — is its slow, maneuvering branch, while the Evans Gambit
offers a pawn to rip the center open. The Italian teaches the beginner's virtues
better than almost any other opening: rapid development and pressure on the
weakest point in the enemy camp.


The Ruy Lopez, or Spanish Opening (1.e4 e5 2.Nf3 Nc6 3.Bb5).

The Spanish bishop pins nothing yet but pressures the knight that defends Black's
center pawn, and the resulting struggle over the e5 point has filled more theory
than any other king-pawn opening. White builds slowly, often retreating the
bishop and maneuvering the queen's knight to the kingside, while Black must solve
the problem of his slightly cramped position. It is the great strategic battlefield
of classical chess, prized for richness rather than for any quick knockout.


The Sicilian Defense (1.e4 c5).

The Sicilian answers the king's pawn not with symmetry but with an unbalancing
flank pawn, fighting for the center from the side and inviting a sharp, double-edged
game. Black accepts a half-open c-file and long-term counterplay in exchange for
ceding some space, and the resulting positions are among the most combative in
chess. It is the choice of the player who wants to win with the black pieces, not
merely to equalize.


The French Defense (1.e4 e6).

The French builds a solid pawn chain and strikes at White's center with the move
d5, accepting a slightly cramped but resilient position. Its one well-known burden
is the queen's bishop, hemmed in behind its own pawns, and much of Black's
middlegame skill lies in freeing or trading that piece. The French rewards
patience and a feel for pawn chains: play on the side where your chain points, and
break at its base.


The Alekhine's Defense (1.e4 Nf6).

The Alekhine invites White to chase the knight with pawns, deliberately letting
White build a broad pawn center in the belief that it can later be undermined and
turned into a weakness. It is named for the player's actual first move and move
order, not for any structure it later resembles, and a position that comes to look
like a French through some other path is still the opening that was actually
played. It is a hypermodern idea: let the opponent occupy the center, then attack
what he has built.


The Queen's Gambit (1.d4 d5 2.c4).

Not truly a gambit, since the offered c-pawn cannot be safely held, the Queen's
Gambit fights for the center by offering to trade a flank pawn for a central one.
Declining it with e6 leads to the solid, classical main lines; accepting it gives
the pawn back for development. It is the cornerstone of queen-pawn play and a
model of how to fight for the center without the sharp races of the king-pawn
openings.


On book moves and when to trust them.

A move that follows established theory for the opening actually being played is a
book move, and it should not be second-guessed merely because an engine shows a
marginal preference for something else. Theory is the distilled experience of
strong players, and in the opening it is usually a safer guide to the resulting
middlegame than a raw evaluation a fraction of a pawn higher. Reserve criticism of
opening play for genuine errors — a real loss of material or a concrete tactic
missed — not for honest deviations within sound theory.
