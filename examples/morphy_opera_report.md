# The Opera Game: Morphy vs. Duke Karl / Count Isouard, Paris 1858

---

## Opening narrative

It is 1858, and the Paris Opéra is staging Bellini's *Norma*. In a private box, two aristocratic chess enthusiasts — Duke Karl of Brunswick and Count Isouard de Vauvenargues — have set up a board and are consulting together against a twenty-one-year-old American visitor. Paul Morphy, already the sensation of the chess world, would rather be watching the opera. What follows is one of the most instructive seventeen moves ever played.

The consulting pair choose the Philidor Defence, a solid but inherently passive system that surrenders the centre in exchange for structural firmness. It is a cautious, almost bureaucratic choice — appropriate, perhaps, for two men managing their position by committee. Morphy meets it with the energy of a man who regards every tempo as a moral argument.

What unfolds is less a chess game than a lecture: on development, on open lines, on the relationship between piece activity and material. The Duke and Count will spend the entire game trying to plug holes while Morphy tears new ones open. The opera in the background is about sacrifice and fate; the chess in the foreground is about the same things.

## Move-by-move walkthrough

**1. e4 e5 2. Nf3 d6** — Morphy seizes the centre; the consultants answer with 1...e5 and then commit to the Philidor with 2...d6. The engine mildly prefers 2...Nc6, but the inaccuracy is negligible at this stage — the real test of the defence comes in the next few moves.

**3. d4** — Morphy immediately challenges the centre, the only principled reaction to Black's passive setup.

### 3...Bg4

The engine flags this as an inaccuracy (cp loss: 36), preferring 3...exd4 or 3...Nd7. The consultants appear to be reaching for active counterplay — pinning the Nf3 and hoping to complicate — but the move is premature. The bishop has no stable future on g4 once White pushes the centre, and removing the defender of e5 before the pawn is secure invites exactly the kind of tactical skewer that Morphy will exploit on the very next move. The psychological reading is clear: the pair wanted to *do something*, to create drama. Morphy would have been perfectly content had they simply recaptured on d4 and traded into an equal struggle. Instead, they handed him a target.

**4. dxe5** — Morphy snaps off the pawn, ripping open the position exactly when Black's bishop is offside.

### 4...Bxf3

Another inaccuracy (cp loss: 74). The engine prefers 4...Nd7, tolerating the awkwardness of the pinned pawn in exchange for keeping the bishop. By capturing on f3, the consultants hand White a powerful queen, doubled pawns that are actually very healthy on e4 and e5 (controlling the centre), and — crucially — they open the f-file while doing absolutely nothing about their own king safety. The best move, 4...Nd7, unpins and prepares ...Bxf3 only after White has committed to exd6, keeping options alive. Bxf3 feels like a decision made to *resolve tension* rather than to find the best move — a very human impulse, and a costly one here.

**5. Qxf3** — Morphy recaptures with the queen, centralising it with tempo. **5...dxe5** — Black recovers the pawn, at least restoring material equilibrium.

### 6. Bc4

The engine prefers 6. Qb3 (eval +1.70 vs. +1.34 for Bc4), threatening both f7 and b7 simultaneously and forcing Black into immediate defensive contortions. Morphy's choice of 6. Bc4 is the more famous move — it places the bishop on the ideal diagonal, pointing at f7 — but it is slightly less forcing. This is one of those moments where Morphy's aesthetic instincts nudge him away from the computer's preferred brutalism. He wants development; he wants the *picture* of a mobilised army. The engine's Qb3 is greedier and meaner. Morphy's Bc4 is principled and beautiful, and only a fraction weaker.

### 6...Nf6

Black develops the knight and attacks the queen, but the engine prefers 6...Qe7 (eval +1.34 vs. +2.10 after Nf6). The queen on e7 would defend e5, keep the king off the back rank somewhat, and avoid opening the g5 diagonal. By playing Nf6 first, the consultants let Morphy install the bishop on g5 next move with maximum effect, pinning the very piece they just developed. It is the kind of inaccuracy born from a "natural" developing instinct — Nf6 looks right, looks active. But natural moves and correct moves are not always the same thing.

**7. Qb3** — Now the queen reaches b3 anyway, threatening f7 and b7 simultaneously. Morphy gets his desired position one move later than ideal, but the consultants have lost a tempo into the bargain.

### 7...Qe7

The engine flags this as an inaccuracy (cp loss: 34), preferring the counterintuitive 7...Bc5, which at least contests the f2–a7 diagonal and forces 8. Bxf7+, after which Black's king wanders to e7 in an ugly but potentially fighting position. With 7...Qe7, the consultants are already in pure damage-limitation mode: the queen defends both f7 and e5, but it passively blocks the f8-bishop and surrenders any pretence of dynamic play. The pair are consulting with each other, and perhaps the instinct to *agree* on a move pushes them toward the safest-looking option rather than the sharpest.

### 8. Nc3

The engine actually prefers 8. Qxb7 here (eval +2.24 vs. +1.78 for Nc3), snatching the b-pawn with 8...Qb4+ 9. Qxb4 Bxb4+ 10. c3, winning material. Morphy instead plays the developing move, completing his minor-piece mobilisation. Once again, his instincts are aesthetic and principled rather than immediately rapacious. He is building an army, not picking pockets. The practical cost is modest — about half a pawn — and his position remains overwhelmingly better.

**8...c6** — The consultants try to stabilise the queenside. **9. Bg5** — Morphy pins the Nf6 to the queen, adding yet another attacker to Black's already strained kingside.

### 9...b5

A lunge — and a serious mistake (cp loss: 89). The engine prefers 9...Qc7, simply defending and hoping to consolidate. With 9...b5, the Duke and Count are attempting a queenside counterattack, trying to drive away the Bc4. It is a bold idea on paper, but it ignores the first rule of defensive chess: when your king is stranded in the centre, do not open more lines. The move has the psychology of desperation dressed as aggression — the consultants sense the position is bad and swing for a tactical blow. Morphy will simply take the pawn and use the new lines against them.

### 10. Nxb5 cxb5

The engine prefers 10...Qb4+ (eval +2.78 vs. +3.63 after cxb5), which at least forces queens off the board and alleviates some pressure. By recapturing with 10...cxb5, the consultants walk into a discovered check and give Morphy the open c-file for his rook. This is the point of no return: every pawn they pushed to chase White's pieces has simply become a target or an opening in their own position.

### 11. Bxb5+

A crisp, thematic move, the engine's best choice. White sacrifices nothing — the bishop is immune — and the check forces Black's king into the open. After 11...Kd8 12. O-O-O+ Kc8, the engine's principal variation shows the rook swinging to d3 with decisive effect. What is notable here is that the most forcing continuation (Qxb5+) would actually be far weaker (+2.06), and simply retreating (Be2) would be a disaster (-1.35). Morphy's choice is not merely best — it is the only move that maintains the initiative.

### 11...Nbd7

The engine prefers 11...Kd8 (eval +4.37 vs. +4.71 after Nbd7). With Kd8, Black at least keeps both knights on the board and reduces the material deficit slightly. The consultants instead interpose with the knight, trying to develop and block the check simultaneously. It is understandable — Kd8 feels ugly, walking into 12. O-O-O+ — but actually Kd8 makes the position slightly more resistant. Nbd7, paradoxically, accelerates White's attack by giving him a piece to exchange off on d7.

**12. O-O-O** — Morphy castles long, rooks instantly connecting and the d-file fully open. **12...Rd8** — The consultants contest the d-file, their only active try.

**13. Rxd7** — Morphy smashes through, exchanging rooks and clearing the path.

### 13...Rxd7

A mistake (cp loss: 104). The engine prefers 13...Nxd7 (eval +4.82 vs. +5.86 after Rxd7), because after 13...Nxd7 14. Bxe7 Bxe7 15. Rd1, Black at least has two pieces on the board and can try to castle. By recapturing with the rook instead of the knight, the consultants leave the Nf6 hanging and hand Morphy a free piece — or so it seems. In fact the real tragedy, as we are about to see, is more complicated. The pair almost certainly played Rxd7 instinctively, maintaining the rook on the seventh rank. It looks active. It is passive.

### 14. Rd1

The engine's only good move here (flagged as such), maintaining the full force of White's initiative. Alternatives collapse the advantage dramatically: 14. Bxf6 (only +2.05) or 14. Bxd7+ (only +1.61) both dissipate the attack. Morphy does not take either the free piece on f6 or the free exchange on d7. He plays the rook to d1 — quiet, threatening, inevitable. The pressure on the d-file is now overwhelming, with the bishop on g5 pinning the Nf6 and the queen on b3 eyeing f7. Black's position is held together by one thread.

**14...Qe6** — The consultants defend the f6-knight and offer a queen trade. It is their only reasonable try.

### 15. Bxd7+

The engine scores this as a blunder (cp loss: 332), preferring 15. Bxf6 (eval +5.90 vs. +2.58 after Bxd7+). The principled continuation is 15. Bxf6 gxf6 16. Bxd7+ Qxd7 17. Qb8+, which is a clean forced win. Instead, Morphy plays 15. Bxd7+, which allows 15...Nxd7 and suddenly the mating net is — on paper — not quite there. The engine drops White's advantage from nearly +6 to +2.58. This is the famous "blunder" in what is remembered as a perfect game. Morphy may have been calculating the mating sequence ahead of time and misordered his moves by one, or perhaps he simply played on feel, trusting that the attack must be decisive. In a game already won by a wide margin, the miscalculation is academic. But it is a reminder that even the games we immortalise are human.

### 15...Nxd7

The engine's evaluation collapses to **#2 for White** — that is, checkmate in two moves. And yet 15...Nxd7 is classified as a blunder (cp loss: 99,740). Why? Because after 15. Bxd7+ the *only* moves that didn't immediately lose were Nxd7 — so it is indeed the best of a terrible set of alternatives. The engine marks it a blunder simply because the position is now objectively lost by force. The consultants may have thought they were finally untangling: the bishop is gone, the knight recaptures, the queen still guards. They could not have seen what was coming.

### 16. Qb8+

The only move that wins (flagged as such), and one of the most famous moves in chess history. The queen sacrifice — 16. Qb8+ Nxb8 17. Rd8# — strips away Black's defensive knight and delivers checkmate with the rook. Without this sequence, White's advantage actually dissolves (16. Qc3 or 16. Qb7 lead to only a modest edge of around +2.7). Morphy almost certainly saw this mate from many moves earlier, and the entire preceding sequence — Rd1, the patient refusal to snatch pieces, the bishop sacrifice on d7 — was the setup for this single moment. The queen steps forward into its own capture not as a miscalculation but as a deliberate gift, the rhetorical flourish at the end of an argument already won.

### 16...Nxb8

Forced. There is no other move. The knight takes the queen and awaits its fate.

### 17. Rd8#

Checkmate. The rook slides to d8, supported by the knight on b8 that has helpfully blocked its own king from escaping. The back rank is sealed. The game is over. The engine notes, with admirable pedantry, that any other White move here would actually *lose* — which is simply the engine's way of acknowledging that Rd8 is the only legal move that delivers mate, and everything else leaves a game still to be played. It is the system confirming what the board makes obvious: there is nothing left to say.

## Closing reflection

This game has been called the Everest of chess miniatures, and with good reason — it is not merely that Morphy wins quickly, but *how* he wins. From move three onward, he never chases material; he mobilises his pieces, opens lines, and castles into attack. The Duke and Count, for their part, are not careless players. They make recognisable decisions at every turn — developing pieces, contesting files, trying counterplay on the queenside. But each of their "natural" moves turns out to cede another tempo, open another file, or misplace another piece. This is the Philidor Defence's original sin played out in miniature: passive setups invite active opponents to dictate the entire course of the game.

The engine's verdict adds one ironic footnote to the legend: Morphy's own 15. Bxd7+ is technically a blunder, and his earlier 8. Nc3 and 6. Bc4 were marginally suboptimal compared to more aggressive alternatives. Yet the game never ceased to be a masterpiece. What the engine shows is that Morphy was not a calculating machine but an artist with overwhelming strategic instincts — he played the *character* of the position correctly even when he misplayed a specific move order. The consultants, meanwhile, were undone not by any single catastrophic error but by a cumulative failure of development, a game-long reluctance to accept the ugly defensive moves that might have prolonged resistance. In the end, the rook lands on d8 and the opera plays on, and it is very hard to imagine Morphy minding either.
